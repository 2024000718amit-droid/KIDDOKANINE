Evidence-Based Principle — Marwah / DCI

Behavior management in Indian children requires accounting for the Joint Family Dynamic — grandparents, aunts, and multiple adults often accompany the child. Establish a single-voice protocol before the child enters the chair. Marwah (5th Ed.) identifies the dentist's voice modulation and non-verbal communication as the most critical variables in non-pharmacological management.

      Frankl 1 — Definitely Negative
      Refuses treatment. Cries forcefully. Extremely negative. Unmanageable with standard NP techniques alone.
      Frankl 2 — Negative
      Reluctant to accept treatment. Some evidence of negative attitude. Not cooperative, sulky, withdrawn.
      Frankl 3 — Positive
      Accepts treatment. Cautious, willing to comply with dentist, follows directions with occasional reserve.
      Frankl 4 — Definitely Positive
      Good rapport. Interested in dental procedures, laughs, enjoys situation. Cooperates fully.
      Wright's classification (1975): cooperative, lacking cooperative ability, potentially cooperative. Frankl (1962) is the most cited in Indian dental colleges (Marwah Ch.12).
      Non-Pharmacological (NP) — First line per Marwah:
      Tell-Show-Do (TSD) Voice Control Positive Reinforcement Distraction Desensitisation Modelling HOME Restraint (Papoose)

      Pharmacological — Escalation per DCI:
      N₂O-O₂ Anxiolysis Oral Midazolam Oral Triclofos GA (Last resort)

      HOME (Hand Over Mouth Exercise): No longer recommended as primary technique per 2022 AAPD guidelines. Use only as absolute last resort NP, with full parental informed consent, and never in medically compromised children. Shobha Tandon includes it with heavy caveats.

      Early Childhood Caries (ECC) — Indian Context (Marwah Ch. 20):

      India has one of the highest ECC prevalence rates globally — studies from AIIMS, KGMCand Manipal show prevalence between 44–65% in pre-school age groups.

      Primary drivers in Indian setting:
      • Prolonged on-demand breastfeeding (bottle or direct) beyond 18 months
      • Sugar-sweetened beverages (packaged juices, cola) from infancy
      • Sharing spoons / kissing — MS transmission from mother
      • Night-time feeding without oral hygiene

      Marwah Classification of ECC severity:
      Type I (mild) — posterior only, diet related
      Type II (moderate) — anterior + posterior
      Type III (severe) — all surfaces, including lower incisors

      SDF (Silver Diamine Fluoride): 38% SDF is now endorsed in Marwah 5th Ed. as a viable interim or definitive treatment for uncooperative young children in Indian settings. Arrest rate: 81% in primary teeth. Cost-effective for lower-resource settings.
      Pulp Therapy Decision Tree (Marwah 5th Ed. Ch.15 + AAPD Pulp Therapy Guidelines 2024):

      Vital Pulp Therapy:
      Indirect Pulp Treatment (IPT) — 1 or 2-step. Selective caries removal, Ca(OH)₂ or MTA base. Primary and permanent teeth. AAPD 2024: 1-step IPT preferred.
      Pulpotomy (primary) — coronal pulp removed. MTA or biodentine preferred agent. Formocresol still acceptable (Marwah). Success rate 94% (MTA).
      Apexogenesis — immature permanent teeth with vital pulp. Preserve Hertwig's sheath. MTA/Biodentine pulpotomy or partial pulpotomy.

      Non-Vital / Necrotic:
      Pulpectomy (primary) — complete pulp removal. Obturate with KRI paste (preferred, Marwah 5th) or ZOE. 2-visit protocol for infected cases.
      Apexification — immature permanent with necrotic pulp. Ca(OH)₂ x 6-18 mo OR MTA apical plug (1-visit). Monitor radiographically.
      Revascularisation / Regenerative Endodontics — immature permanent, open apex, necrotic pulp. AAPD 2024 endorsed. Blood clot scaffold + MTA/Biodentine coronal plug. Long-term root development possible.

      Contraindications to vital pulp therapy (Marwah): spontaneous/nocturnal pain, pathologic root resorption (>1/3), periapical rarefaction, furcation involvement, mobility Grade III. If any present → pulpectomy or extraction.
      MIH — Molar Incisor Hypomineralisation (EAPD Definition + Marwah 5th Ed. Ch.21 + AAPD 2024):

      Qualitative enamel defect affecting ≥1 first permanent molar (FPM), often with incisor involvement. Prevalence in India: 9–12% (JISPPD studies). Cause: systemic insult during enamel mineralisation (late pregnancy–age 3).

      EAPD Severity Index (per tooth):
      Score 0 No demarcated opacity
      Score 1 Demarcated opacity (white/yellow/brown) — intact surface
      Score 2 Post-eruptive enamel breakdown
      Score 3 Atypical restoration / extraction history

      Management by severity:
      Score 1 → Fluoride varnish + GIC sealant + monitor
      Score 2 (small) → Resin infiltration (Icon) or composite bonding
      Score 2 (large) → SSC for FPMs, composite for incisors
      Score 3 → SSC or extraction (if poor prognosis) + orthodontic assessment

      LA note: MIH FPMs are hypersensitive — standard block may be inadequate. Use articaine 4% or intraligamentary supplemental injection. Warn patient before any air/water on affected tooth.

      Fluoride Protocol — AAPD 2024 / Marwah 5th Ed. Ch.7 / WHO / Indian Context:

      Water Fluoride — India: WHO optimal level 0.7–1.0 ppm. Most Indian municipal water: 0.1–0.3 ppm (deficient). Rajasthan, AP, Telangana, Gujarat: endemic fluorosis zones (2–10+ ppm).

      Fluoride Supplements (where water <0.3 ppm — AAPD):
      Age 6 mo–3 yr: 0.25 mg/day · Age 3–6 yr: 0.50 mg/day · Age 6–16 yr: 1.0 mg/day

      Toothpaste:
      Age 0–2: smear (rice grain) 1000 ppm — if high caries risk
      Age 2–6: pea-sized (500–1000 ppm) — do not rinse after brushing
      Age 6+: pea-sized 1000–1450 ppm — standard adult toothpaste

      Fluoride Varnish (5% NaF = 22,600 ppm): AAPD 2024: apply every 3–6 months for moderate-high risk. Method: dry tooth, apply thin layer with brush/applicator, instruct no eating/drinking 30 min. Safe even if swallowed (0.25 mL = ~2.5 mg F).

      SDF 38% (44,800 ppm F + 254,000 ppm Ag): Apply annually. Arrest rate 81% primary teeth. Staining mandatory counselling.

      Pit & Fissure Sealants: AAPD 2024: resin-based sealants preferred. RMGIC sealants acceptable. Apply to all erupted permanent molars in moderate-high risk children. Marwah: RMGIC sealants have additional F-release benefit for high-risk Indian children.
